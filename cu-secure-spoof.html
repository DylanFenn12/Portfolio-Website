CU Secure Wi-Fi Spoof Poster

A convincing counterfeit of a publicly displayed campus Wi-Fi poster — recreated from scratch — then subtly rewritten to flip the message into a “wait… are we sure this is fine?” moment. Designed to test recreation skills and explore authority through design.

Typography mimicry Layout + scale matching Subversion + satire Placement documentation

Overview

Using the design skills and judgement built in the course, I selected a publicly displayed campus poster with a clear, authoritative message. I rebuilt it from scratch (no “slap a rectangle over the text” shortcuts), matching fonts, spacing, hierarchy, and print feel. Then I modified the copy to subvert the original intent to keep it believable, but let the new message sneak up on the viewer.

Why this target?

It’s copy-able, highly visible, and it relies on bright colors to convince you of it's realism. That made it perfect for a subtle “authority remix” where the form stays the same but the meaning flips.

Process

Rules I followed

  • Publicly displayed target: a campus poster.
  • Rebuilt from scratch: replicated layout/type/image placement carefully.
  • Subtle, relevant change: not a dramatic redesign, but a believable message twist.
  • Documented placement: installed + photographed in context.
Crime Level: I think the IT office hates me now.

Reflection

Message, modification, and connection to subversion.

The original poster communicates authority through consistency: institutional branding, clean hierarchy, and instruction-like language. It assumes compliance and frames the connection process as routine and harmless. My counterfeit maintains that same “official” visual logic while altering the copy to highlight the tension between convenience and surveillance/trust. The subversion is intentionally subtle: the format remains credible, but the rewritten message reframes the situation so viewers reconsider what they’re agreeing to and who benefits. In the context of class themes (power, authority, vandalism, subversion), the work uses the poster’s own design language as the delivery mechanism. The intervention isn’t about destroying the original, it’s about borrowing its legitimacy to expose how legitimacy can be manufactured.

Takeaway

If the design looks official enough, most people will believe it. Which is… funny. And also not funny.
